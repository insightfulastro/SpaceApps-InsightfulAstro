Mercury

Equatorial Diameter: 4,880 km
Mass: 3.30×10^23 kg (0.055 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 57.9 million km (0.39 AU)
Rotation Period: 59 Earth days
Solar Orbit Period: 88 Earth days
Surface Gravity: 3.7 m/s² (0.38 g)
Surface Temperature: -173 to 427°C
Fun Facts:
Mercury has a very thin atmosphere, which is composed mostly of oxygen, sodium, and hydrogen.
It has the most eccentric (oval-shaped) orbit of all the planets in the solar system.

Venus

Equatorial Diameter: 12,104 km
Mass: 4.87×10^24 kg (0.815 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 108.2 million km (0.72 AU)
Rotation Period: 243 Earth days (retrograde rotation)
Solar Orbit Period: 225 Earth days
Surface Gravity: 8.87 m/s² (0.9 g)
Surface Temperature: 471°C (constant)
Fun Facts:
Venus rotates backward compared to most planets, meaning the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east.
Venus is the hottest planet in the solar system due to its thick atmosphere, composed mainly of carbon dioxide, trapping heat.

Earth

Equatorial Diameter: 12,742 km
Mass: 5.97×10^24
5.97×10^24 kg (1 Earth)
Mean Distance from Sun: 149.6 million km (1 AU)
Rotation Period: 24 hours
Solar Orbit Period: 365.25 Earth days
Surface Gravity: 9.8 m/s² (1 g)
Surface Temperature: -88 to 58°C
Fun Facts:
Earth is the only known planet to support life, with a perfect balance of water, atmosphere, and climate.
About 70% of Earth's surface is covered by oceans, the largest surface water area in the solar system..

Mars

Equatorial Diameter: 6,779 km
Mass: 6.42×10^23 kg (0.107 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 227.9 million km (1.52 AU)
Rotation Period: 24.6 hours
Solar Orbit Period: 687 Earth days
Surface Gravity: 3.71 m/s² (0.38 g)
Surface Temperature: -125 to 20°C
Fun Facts:
Mars has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, which is about 3 times the height of Mount Everest.
The Martian atmosphere is primarily carbon dioxide, with traces of nitrogen and argon, and it is too thin to support liquid water on its surface for long.

Jupiter

Equatorial Diameter: 139,820 km
Mass: 1.90×10^27 kg (318 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 778.3 million km (5.2 AU)
Rotation Period: 9.9 hours
Solar Orbit Period: 11.86 Earth years
Surface Gravity: 24.79 m/s² (2.53 g)
Surface Temperature: -108°C
Fun Facts:
Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field of all the planets and is known for its Great Red Spot, a giant storm larger than Earth that has been raging for centuries.
Jupiter has at least 79 moons, including the four largest (Galilean moons): Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Saturn

Equatorial Diameter: 116,460 km
Mass: 5.68×10^26 kg (95.16 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 1.43 billion km (9.58 AU)
Rotation Period: 10.7 hours
Solar Orbit Period: 29.45 Earth years
Surface Gravity: 10.44 m/s² (1.06 g)
Surface Temperature: -139°C
Fun Facts:
Saturn is famous for its large and complex ring system, made mostly of ice particles, rock, and dust.
Despite its size, Saturn is the least dense of all the planets and would float in water if a large enough body of water existed.

Uranus

Equatorial Diameter: 50,724 km
Mass: 8.68×10^25 kg (14.54 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 2.87 billion km (19.22 AU)
Rotation Period: 17.2 hours (retrograde rotation)
Solar Orbit Period: 84 Earth years
Surface Gravity: 8.69 m/s² (0.89 g)
Surface Temperature: -195°C
Fun Facts:
Uranus rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of about 98°, making its seasons extremely long.
It has faint rings and at least 27 moons, and is often called an “ice giant” due to its cold atmosphere composed of hydrogen, helium, and methane.

Neptune

Equatorial Diameter: 49,244 km
Mass: 1.02×10^26 kg (17.15 Earths)
Mean Distance from Sun: 4.5 billion km (30.07 AU)
Rotation Period: 16.1 hours
Solar Orbit Period: 164.79 Earth years
Surface Gravity: 11.15 m/s² (1.14 g)
Surface Temperature: -201°C
Fun Facts:
Neptune has the fastest winds in the solar system, reaching speeds up to 2,100 km/h.
It was the first planet discovered through mathematical predictions rather than direct observation.
